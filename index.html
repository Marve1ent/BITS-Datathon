CIVS — Formula Reference

The College Investment Viability Score (CIVS) is a weighted linear combination of five min-max–normalized components capturing payoff magnitude, payoff latency, failure robustness, downside protection, and structural completion probability. Heterogeneous financial and temporal variables are mapped onto a common [0, 1] scale before aggregation, enabling consistent cross-program comparisons that account for payoff magnitude, latency, and failure risk.

Master Equation

CIVS = w₁·Ñ(C1) + w₂·Ñ(C2) + w₃·Ñ(C3) + w₄·Ñ(C4) + w₅·Ñ(C5)

Ñ(Cᵢ) = min-max normalization of component Cᵢ onto [0, 1]

Weights (sum to 1.0):
  w₁ = 0.35 — C1: Lifetime ROI (payoff magnitude)
  w₂ = 0.20 — C2: Time to positive ROI (payoff latency, inverted)
  w₃ = 0.20 — C3: Completion robustness (failure sensitivity)
  w₄ = 0.15 — C4: Downside protection (dropout floor vs. baseline)
  w₅ = 0.10 — C5: Graduation probability (structural success rate)

CIVS × 100 → score on 0–100 scale
≥ 70 → ✅ INVEST | 45–69 → ⚠️ CAUTION | < 45 → 🔴 RECONSIDER
By normalizing heterogeneous variables onto a common scale before aggregation, CIVS ensures no single dimension dominates due to unit or scale differences (e.g., raw dollars vs. years vs. ratios). The result is a decision-oriented, reproducible evaluation framework suitable for scalable cross-program analysis.

Normalization Bounds

Ñ(Cᵢ) = ( Cᵢ − min_i ) / ( max_i − min_i ), clamped to [0, 1]

Component min max Direction
──────────────────────────────────────────────
C1 ROI % −100% +400% higher = better
C2 Years 1 40 lower = better → Ñ(C2) = 1 − normalize(C2)
C3 Ratio 0.0 1.0 higher = better
C4 Ratio −1.0 1.0 higher = better
C5 Prob 0.0 1.0 higher = better
Bounds are empirically grounded on realistic college ROI scenarios. C2 is inverted before weighting — a shorter path to positive ROI signals stronger viability.

C1 — Lifetime ROI  w₁ = 0.35

C1 = ( EarningsPremium − NetCost ) / NetCost × 100

EarningsPremium = [ (W_start×10 + W_mid×15 + W_late×15)
               − (N_start×10 + N_mid×15 + N_late×15) ] × (1.02)^(yrs/2)

NetCost = DirectCosts − Aid − FamilyContrib + OppCost + LoanInterest
   OppCost = $32,000 × years_enrolled
   LoanInterest: monthly payment via annuity → P = L × [r(1+r)^n/((1+r)^n−1)]
The highest weight (35%) because payoff magnitude is the primary determinant of investment quality. Three career phases model earnings across early (yrs 1–10), mid (11–25), and late (26–40) career, adjusted for 2% annual growth. No-degree baseline is field-specific from BLS data.
Inputs: sal_start, sal_mid, total_cost, aid, family, loans, grad_years, major

C2 — Time to Positive ROI  w₂ = 0.20, inverted

C2_raw = NetCost / ( StartSalary_WITH − StartSalary_WITHOUT )

Ñ(C2) = 1 − [ (C2_raw − 1) / (40 − 1) ] ← inverted normalization

Examples: 5 yrs → Ñ ≈ 0.90 | 15 yrs → Ñ ≈ 0.64 | 40 yrs → Ñ = 0.0
Payoff latency matters independently of total magnitude. A degree that breaks even in 30 years is structurally riskier than one that breaks even in 7, even if lifetime totals are similar. This component captures that timing dimension explicitly.
Inputs: total_cost, aid, family, loans, sal_start, major (for no-degree baseline)

C3 — Completion Robustness  w₃ = 0.20

GradProb = 0.60 (BLS national 6-yr baseline)
   + 0.05 if major certainty ≥ 4
   + 0.05 if career requires degree
   − 0.05 if first-generation student
GradProb clamped to [0.30, 0.95]

DropoutROI = ( EarnNoDeg − DropoutCost ) / DropoutCost × 100
   DropoutCost = DirectCosts/2 + OppCost/2

RiskWeightedROI = GradProb × OnTimeROI + (1−GradProb) × DropoutROI

C3 = RiskWeightedROI / OnTimeROI , clamped [0, 1]
Measures what fraction of the on-time ROI survives after weighting by the real-world probability of not finishing. C3 near 1.0 = return is robust to dropout risk. C3 near 0 = return is fragile and highly completion-dependent.
Inputs: major_cert, career_req, firstgen

C4 — Downside Protection  w₄ = 0.15

DropoutROI = ( EarnNoDeg − DropoutCost ) / DropoutCost × 100
NoDegBaseline = 100 (proxy for zero-investment no-degree career return)

C4 = DropoutROI / NoDegBaseline , clamped to [−1, 1]

Ñ(C4) = ( C4 + 1 ) / 2

C4 > 0: partial enrollment still beats not enrolling at all
C4 < 0: dropout leaves you worse off than the no-college counterfactual
Measures whether the investment has a floor. A positive C4 means even a dropout scenario produces better economic outcomes than never enrolling. A negative C4 signals no downside protection — the investment fails catastrophically if not completed.
Derived from: total_cost, grad_years, major (no-degree earnings baseline)

C5 — Graduation Probability  w₅ = 0.10

C5 = GradProb (same adjusted value as C3)

Base rate: 0.60 (BLS 6-year national completion rate)
Adjustments: +0.05 / +0.05 / −0.05 (same as C3)
Clamped to [0.30, 0.95]

Ñ(C5) = C5 (bounds are [0, 1]; normalization is identity here)
Enters CIVS as a standalone term to reward structural conditions associated with completion (clear major, career-aligned field, support networks). While C3 captures ROI sensitivity to dropout risk, C5 captures the absolute probability independently — they are conceptually distinct even when they share the same inputs.
Inputs: major_cert, career_req, firstgen

Worked Example — Public In-State STEM Student

$120K total cost, $40K loans, $55K starting salary, 4-year program:
C1 (ROI %) = +185% → Ñ = (185+100)/(400+100) = 0.57
C2 (Break-even) = 9 yrs → Ñ = 1 − (9−1)/(40−1) = 0.79
C3 (Robustness) = 0.71 → Ñ = 0.71
C4 (Downside) = 0.42 → Ñ = (0.42+1)/2 = 0.71
C5 (Grad prob) = 0.65 → Ñ = 0.65

CIVS = 0.35×0.57 + 0.20×0.79 + 0.20×0.71 + 0.15×0.71 + 0.10×0.65
= 0.200 + 0.158 + 0.142 + 0.107 + 0.065
= 0.672 → CIVS = 67.2 → ⚠️ CAUTION
